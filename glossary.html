Glossary of UK Political Terms

A reference guide to the key terms and concepts used in UK politics, from electoral systems to parliamentary procedure.

26 terms found

Constituency

ElectionsBeginner

A geographical area that elects one MP to Parliament. The UK is divided into 650 constituencies, each roughly equal in population.

Example: Your local MP represents your constituency in Parliament.

Parliament

ParliamentBeginner

The group of people who make and change laws in the UK. It has two parts: the House of Commons (elected MPs) and the House of Lords (appointed members).

Example: Parliament meets at the Palace of Westminster in London.

MP (Member of Parliament)

ParliamentBeginner

An elected representative who sits in the House of Commons and speaks for their constituency. There are 650 MPs in total.

House of Commons

ParliamentBeginner

The elected chamber of Parliament where laws are debated and voted on. The party with the most MPs usually forms the government.

House of LordsMP (Member of Parliament)Parliament

House of Lords

ParliamentBeginner

The unelected chamber of Parliament that reviews and suggests changes to laws. Members are appointed, not voted for.

Example: The House of Lords can delay a bill but cannot block it permanently.

General Election

ElectionsBeginner

A national vote where all 650 constituencies elect their MP. General elections happen at least every five years, and the winning party forms the government.

Example: The next general election must be held by January 2025 at the latest.

By-election

ElectionsBeginner

A local election held in a single constituency when its MP resigns, dies, or is removed. Only that constituency votes.

Example: A by-election was called after the MP stepped down due to ill health.

General ElectionConstituencyMP (Member of Parliament)

First Past the Post (FPTP)

VotingBeginner

The voting system used in UK general elections. The candidate with the most votes in each constituency wins — you do not need more than half the votes.

Example: Under FPTP, a candidate can win with 35% of the vote if the other candidates split the remaining 65%.

Majority

ElectionsIntermediate

The difference in votes or seats between the winning candidate or party and the runner-up. A majority government holds more than half of all seats (at least 326).

Example: The government has a majority of 80 seats, meaning it can pass laws without support from other parties.

Majority GovernmentHung ParliamentMarginal Seat

Majority Government

GovernmentBeginner

When one party wins more than half of all seats in Parliament, allowing it to pass laws without needing support from other parties.

Example: A majority government can pass its manifesto pledges more easily than a minority government.

Hung ParliamentMinority GovernmentCoalition

Hung Parliament

GovernmentBeginner

When no single party wins more than half the seats. The largest party may form a coalition government or run as a minority government.

Example: The 2010 election resulted in a hung parliament, leading to a coalition between the Conservatives and Liberal Democrats.

CoalitionMinority GovernmentMajority Government

Coalition

GovernmentBeginner

An agreement between two or more parties to share power and form a government together, usually because no single party won enough seats on its own.

Example: A coalition government requires the parties to agree on a shared set of policies.

Hung ParliamentMinority GovernmentMajority Government

Minority Government

GovernmentIntermediate

A government formed by the largest party even though it has fewer than half the seats. It relies on other parties to support each new law.

Example: A minority government must negotiate with other parties to pass its budget.

Hung ParliamentCoalitionMajority Government

Manifesto

PartiesBeginner

A document published by a political party before an election, listing the promises and policies it will carry out if it wins.

Example: The party launched its manifesto with pledges on health, education, and tax.

Cabinet

GovernmentBeginner

A group of senior ministers chosen by the Prime Minister to lead government departments. They meet regularly to make key decisions.

Example: The Chancellor of the Exchequer is one of the most senior members of the Cabinet.

Prime MinisterPrime Minister's Questions (PMQs)

Prime Minister

GovernmentBeginner

The head of the UK government, usually the leader of the party with the most MPs. They appoint ministers and set the government's direction.

Example: The Prime Minister lives and works at 10 Downing Street.

Whip

ParliamentIntermediate

A party official who makes sure MPs attend votes and follow the party line. Breaking the whip can lead to losing the party's support.

Example: The chief whip sent a three-line whip, requiring all MPs to attend and vote with the party.

MP (Member of Parliament)Backbencher

Backbencher

ParliamentIntermediate

An MP who does not hold a government or opposition leadership role. They sit on the back benches and vote according to their party or conscience.

Example: Backbenchers can rebel against their party by voting against the whip.

MP (Member of Parliament)Whip

Royal Assent

ParliamentIntermediate

The final step where the monarch formally approves a bill, turning it into law. This is a ceremonial step — no monarch has refused since 1708.

Example: Once Royal Assent is granted, a bill becomes an Act of Parliament.

ParliamentHouse of Lords

Devolution

GovernmentBeginner

The transfer of power from the UK Parliament to local parliaments and assemblies in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland, allowing them to make their own laws on some issues.

Example: The Scottish Parliament controls health and education policy in Scotland.

Parliament

Referendum

VotingIntermediate

A nationwide vote on a single question. The result is not automatically binding but is usually followed by the government.

Example: The 2016 EU referendum asked: 'Should the UK remain a member of the EU?'

General ElectionDevolution

Swing

ElectionsIntermediate

A measure of how many voters moved from one party to another between elections. Often used to predict which seats might change hands.

Example: A 5% swing from Labour to Conservative would mean many marginal seats change hands.

Marginal SeatGeneral Election

Marginal Seat

ElectionsIntermediate

A constituency where the winning candidate won by a small margin, making it likely to switch to another party at the next election.

Example: Parties focus their campaign efforts on marginal seats.

Safe SeatSwingConstituency

Safe Seat

ElectionsIntermediate

A constituency where one party always wins by a large margin, making it very unlikely to change hands at an election.

Example: The incumbent has held this safe seat for over 30 years.

King's Speech

ParliamentIntermediate

A speech read by the monarch at the opening of Parliament, setting out the government's plans for the coming year. The speech is written by the government, not the king.

Example: The King's Speech outlined 35 new bills for the upcoming parliamentary session.

ParliamentPrime Minister

Prime Minister's Questions (PMQs)

ParliamentBeginner

A weekly session every Wednesday where the Prime Minister answers questions from MPs. It is the most watched event in Parliament.

Example: PMQs is broadcast live on television every Wednesday at midday.
