Glossary of UK Political Terms

A reference guide to the key terms and concepts used in UK politics, from electoral systems to parliamentary procedure.

First Past the Post (FPTP)

The electoral system used for UK general elections. The candidate with the most votes in each constituency wins, even if they don't secure a majority of votes cast.

Hung Parliament

When no single party wins a majority of seats (at least 326) in the House of Commons. This can lead to a coalition government or a minority government.

Coalition Government

A government formed by two or more parties working together, usually because no single party won a majority of seats in a general election.

Minority Government

A government formed by the largest party without a majority, relying on confidence-and-supply agreements or ad-hoc support from other parties to pass legislation.

Whip

A party official responsible for ensuring that MPs attend and vote according to the party line. The 'whip' also refers to the weekly instructions sent to MPs about upcoming votes.

Backbencher

An MP who does not hold a ministerial or shadow ministerial position and sits on the backbenches of the House of Commons.

Frontbencher

An MP who holds a ministerial position in government or a shadow ministerial position in opposition, sitting on the front benches of the House of Commons.

Prime Minister's Questions (PMQs)

A weekly session in the House of Commons where the Prime Minister answers questions from MPs. Typically held every Wednesday at midday.

King's Speech

A speech delivered by the monarch at the State Opening of Parliament, outlining the government's legislative agenda for the upcoming session.

Select Committee

A cross-party committee of MPs that scrutinises the work of a specific government department, examining policy, spending, and administration.

Public Bill

A bill that proposes changes to the general law of the land, introduced by a government minister or a backbench MP.

Royal Assent

The final stage of the legislative process where the monarch formally approves a bill, making it an Act of Parliament and part of law.

Devolution

The transfer of legislative powers from the UK Parliament to national parliaments and assemblies in Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.

Mandate

The authority granted by voters to a party or government to implement its manifesto commitments after winning an election.

Swing

A measure of voter movement between parties. Calculated as the average of the percentage change in vote share for two parties (e.g., the swing from Conservative to Labour).

Marginal Seat

A constituency where the winning candidate's majority is small, making it likely to change hands at the next election. Key targets for opposition parties.

Safe Seat

A constituency where the winning party holds a large majority and is highly unlikely to lose at a general election.

Recall Petition

A mechanism allowing constituents to force a by-election if their MP is convicted of an offence or suspended from the House. Triggered when 10% of eligible voters sign.
